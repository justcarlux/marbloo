# Simple Tenses:

Simple tenses are the most basic forms of verbs in English. They are used to describe actions or states without adding auxiliary verbs (like "be" or "have") to indicate a continuous or completed aspect. They focus on the fact that an action happens, happened, or will happen, without specifying its duration or completion in relation to other events.

## Simple Present Tense:

The simple present is the base form of the verb. For third-person singular (he, she, it), we add an "\-s" or "\-es" to the end (e.g., plays, talks).

This tense is used to express facts or general truths that are always or generally true, such as stating that plants need sunlight to grow. It is also the tense we use to describe our habits and routines, like a person making their breakfast every day. Additionally, the simple present describes states of being, feelings, or possessions that are true in the current moment, for example, expressing that you live on a mountain.

For example:

- Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius.
- She walks to work every day.
- They live in a small apartment.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the simple present tense, you use the auxiliary verb "do" or "does" (for third-person singular) followed by "not" and the base form of the main verb. You can also use contractions (don't, doesn't).

For example:

- Water doesn't boil at 100 degrees Celsius.
- She doesn't walk to work every day.
- They don't live in a small apartment.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the simple present tense, you use the auxiliary verb "do" or "does" before the subject, followed by the base form of the main verb.

For example:

- Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius → Does water boil at 100 degrees Celsius?
- She walks to work every day → Does she walk to work every day?
- They live in a small apartment → Do they live in a small apartment?

## Simple Past Tense:

The simple past is formed by adding "\-ed" to the base form of regular verbs (e.g., talked, played). Irregular verbs have unique past forms that must be memorized (e.g., go → went, eat → ate).

We use the simple past tense to talk about actions that started and finished at a specific time in the past, such as visiting a grandmother last weekend or buying a new car yesterday. It is also the correct tense to describe past habits or states that were true for a period of time but are no longer true now, like remembering that you played outside every day when you were a child.

For example:

- The movie started exactly at 7 o'clock.
- She wrote a beautiful poem for her mother's birthday.
- We went to the beach during vacation.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the simple past tense, you use the auxiliary verb "did" + "not" + the base form of the main verb. The main verb does not take the past tense form because "did" already carries the past meaning.

For example:

- The movie did not start at 7 o'clock.
- She didn't write a poem for her mother.
- We did not go to the beach during vacation.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the simple past tense, you use the auxiliary verb "did" before the subject, followed by the base form of the main verb.

For example:

- The movie started at 7 o'clock → Did the movie start at 7 o'clock?
- She wrote a poem for her mother → Did she write a poem for her mother?
- They went to the beach during vacation → Did they go to the beach during vacation?

## Simple Future Tense:

The simple future is most commonly formed using the modal auxiliary verb "will" + the base form of the verb (e.g., will go, will see). It can also be formed with "am/is/are going to" + the base verb, especially for plans.

The simple future tense is used to make predictions or guesses about the future based on opinion or knowledge, such as thinking it will rain tomorrow. It is also employed for making spontaneous decisions at the exact moment of speaking, like offering to answer a ringing phone.

For example:

- The company will release a new product next month.
- She is going to study engineering at university.
- We will run out of gas if we don't find a station.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the simple future tense, you add "not" after the auxiliary verb "will" (creating the contraction "won't"). For the "going to" form, you add "not" after the "to be" verb (e.g., "am not," "isn't," "aren't").

For example:

- The company will not release a new product next month.
- She is not going to study engineering at university.
- We will not run out of gas if we find a station soon.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the simple future tense, you invert the subject and the auxiliary verb. For "will," this means placing "will" before the subject. For the "going to" form, you invert the "to be" verb with the subject.

For example:

- The company will release a new product next month → Will the company release a new product next month?
- She is going to study engineering at university → Is she going to study engineering at university?
- We will run out of gas if we find a station → Will we run out of gas if we don't find a station?
