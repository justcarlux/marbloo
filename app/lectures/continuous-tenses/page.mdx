# Continuous Tenses

Continuous (also called progressive) tenses are used to describe actions that are in progress at a specific moment in time. They are formed using a form of the auxiliary verb "be" + the main verb ending in "_\-ing_" (present participle). Unlike simple tenses, continuous tenses emphasize the duration or ongoing nature of an action.

## Present Continuous Tense:

The present continuous is formed with am/is/are + verb(-ing) (e.g., am working, is studying, are playing).

This tense is used to describe actions happening right now, at the moment of speaking, such as talking to a friend or writing a message. It is also used for temporary situations that are happening around the present time but may not be permanent, like living in a city for a short period. Additionally, the present continuous can describe planned future arrangements, especially when a specific time is mentioned.

For example:

- She is reading a book right now.
- She is staying with her family this week.
- I am meeting my teacher tomorrow afternoon.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the present continuous tense, you add "not" after the form of the verb "to be" (am/is/are). Contractions like "isn't" or "aren't" are common.

For example:

- She is not reading a book right now.
- She isn't staying with her family this week.
- I am not meeting my teacher tomorrow afternoon.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the present continuous tense, you invert the subject and the form of the verb "to be" (am/is/are), placing the "to be" verb at the beginning of the sentence.

For example:

- She is reading a book right now → Is she reading a book right now?
- She is staying with her family this week → Is she staying with her family this week?
- I am meeting my teacher afternoon → Am I meeting my teacher tomorrow afternoon?

## Past Continuous Tense:

The past continuous is formed with was/were + verb(-ing) (e.g., was walking, were watching).

We use the past continuous to describe an action that was in progress at a specific moment in the past. It often shows that an action was happening when another action interrupted it. It can also describe two actions that were happening at the same time in the past.

For example:

- I was doing my homework when you called.
- They were playing soccer at 6 p.m. yesterday.
- She was cooking while he was setting the table.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the past continuous tense, you add "not" after the form of the verb "to be" (was/were). The contractions "wasn't" and "weren't" are frequently used.

For example:

- I was not doing my homework when you called.
- They were not playing soccer at 6 p.m. yesterday.
- She was not cooking while he was setting the table.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the past continuous tense, you invert the subject and the form of the verb "to be" (was/were), placing the "to be" verb at the beginning of the sentence.

For example:

- I was doing my homework when you called → Was I doing my homework when you called?
- They were playing soccer at 6 p.m. yesterday → Were they playing soccer at 6 p.m. yesterday?
- She was cooking while he was setting the table → Was she cooking while he was setting the table?

## Future Continuous Tense:

The future continuous is formed with will be + verb(-ing) (e.g., will be traveling, will be studying).

This tense is used to describe an action that will be in progress at a specific time in the future. It can also be used to politely ask about someone's plans or to predict what will be happening at a certain moment.

For example:

- I will be flying to New York tomorrow.
- She will be studying at 8 p.m. tonight.
- Will you be using the computer later?

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the future continuous tense, you add "not" after the auxiliary verb "will," creating the contraction "won't." The structure is will + not + be + verb-ing.

For example:

- I will not be flying to New York tomorrow.
- She will not be studying at 8 p.m. tonight.
- I will not be using the computer later.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the future continuous tense, you invert the subject and the auxiliary verb "will," placing "will" at the beginning of the sentence before the subject.

For example:

- I will be flying to New York tomorrow → Will you be flying to New York tomorrow?
- She will be studying at 8 p.m. today → Will she be studying at 8 p.m. tonight?
- You will be using the computer later → Will you be using the computer later?
