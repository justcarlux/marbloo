# Perfect Continuous Tenses

Perfect continuous tenses combine the ideas of duration (continuous) and completion/connection in time (perfect). They are used to emphasize how long an action has been happening up to a certain point in time.

They are formed using: have/has/had/will have + been + verb(-ing)

## Present Perfect Continuous Tense:

The present perfect continuous is formed with have/has been + verb(-ing) (e.g., have been working, has been studying).

This tense is used to describe actions that started in the past and are still continuing now, or have recently stopped but still have visible results in the present. The focus is usually on the duration of the action.

For example:

- She has been studying for three hours.
- They've been playing soccer since the afternoon.
- I have been feeling tired lately.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the present perfect continuous tense, you add "not" after the auxiliary verb "have" or "has." The structure is have/has + not + been + verb-ing.

For example:

- She has not been studying for three hours.
- They haven't been playing soccer since the afternoon.
- I have not been feeling tired lately.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the present perfect continuous tense, you invert the subject and the auxiliary verb "have" or "has," placing it at the beginning of the sentence before the subject.

For example:

- She has been studying for three hours → Has she been studying for three hours?
- They have been playing soccer since the afternoon → Have they been playing soccer since the afternoon?
- You have been feeling tired lately → Have you been feeling tired lately?

## Past Perfect Continuous Tense:

The past perfect continuous is formed with had been + verb(-ing) (e.g., had been waiting, had been working).

We use this tense to describe an action that had been continuing for some time before another action happened in the past. It emphasizes how long the action was happening before something else occurred.

For example:

- I had been waiting for an hour when the bus finally arrived.
- She had been working all day before she went home.
- They had been traveling for months before they returned.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the past perfect continuous tense, you add "not" after the auxiliary verb "had. The structure is had + not + been + verb-ing.

For example:

- I had not been waiting for an hour when the bus finally arrived.
- She had not been working all day before she went home.
- They hadn't been traveling for months before they returned.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the past perfect continuous tense, you invert the subject and the auxiliary verb "had," placing it at the beginning of the sentence before the subject.

For example:

- I had been waiting for an hour when the bus finally arrived → Had I been waiting for an hour when the bus finally arrived?
- She had been working all day before she went home → Had she been working all day before she went home?
- They had been traveling for months before they returned → Had they been traveling for months before they returned?

## Future Perfect Continuous Tense:

The future perfect continuous is formed with will have been + verb(-ing) (e.g., will have been studying, will have been living).

This tense is used to describe an action that will have been continuing for a specific amount of time before a certain moment in the future. It emphasizes the duration of the action up to that future point.

For example:

- By next month, I will have been working here for five years.
- She will have been studying for three hours by 8 p.m.
- They will have been living in that house for a decade by 2030.

#### Making negative statements:

To form negative statements in the future perfect continuous tense, you add "not" after the auxiliary verb "will". The structure is will + not + have been + verb-ing.

For example:

- By next month, I will not have been working here for five years.
- She will not have been studying for three hours by 8 p.m.
- They will not have been living in that house for a decade by 2030.

#### Making questions:

To form questions in the future perfect continuous tense, you invert the subject and the auxiliary verb "will," placing "will" at the beginning of the sentence before the subject. The structure becomes Will + subject + have been + verb-ing?

For example:

- I will have been working here for five years by next month → Will you have been working here for five years by next month?
- She will have been studying for three hours by 8 p.m. → Will she have been studying for three hours by 8 p.m.?
- They will have been living in that house for a decade by 2030 → Will they have been living in that house for a decade by 2030?
